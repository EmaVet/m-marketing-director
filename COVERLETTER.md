# From "Not a Developer" to GitHub User in a Few Weeks

I didn't expect to end up on GitHub.

I'm not a developer. I've never written production code. I've spent the last few weeks teaching myself how to build things with AI — no-code tools, workflows, automations, and products that solve actual problems. Then suddenly I was signing up for GitHub, among other developer tools, because I needed it to publish what I was building.

Then, during my usual LinkedIn career check-in search, I saw this role and was like... ummm if I'm a GitHub user now, does that make me a developer, or a new kind of user entirely?

I've spent 17 years in marketing leading teams across SaaS, partnerships, community, and growth. Until recently, I would never have considered myself part of GitHub's audience. But here I am.

If that's happening to me, it's happening to a lot of people. And I'm wondering how GitHub is preparing for that.

AI is creating an entirely new type of user. People who aren't developers in the traditional sense, but who are curious enough to learn, ambitious enough to experiment, and practical enough to want to build things themselves instead of waiting for someone else to do it.

At the same time, AI is dramatically lowering the barrier to entry. The gap between "I have an idea" and "I can build this" is collapsing faster than most companies realise.

Which means GitHub's market hasn't just grown. It has fundamentally changed.

That change could have a real impact on APAC and how GitHub goes to market. We now have huge populations of operators, founders, marketers, small business owners, students, and teams all experimenting with AI in real time. Many of them are entering technical ecosystems for the first time (like me). Not because they suddenly want to become engineers, but because building is becoming part of modern work and curiosity is contagious.

And honestly, what an interesting marketing challenge to be part of. Not because I've spent my career marketing to developers — I haven't. But because I understand audiences in transition and technology that disrupts, and that's exciting.

I've built marketing programs for nonprofits learning to trust technology, founders trying to scale communities, and teams navigating entirely new ways of working. I know how to meet people where they are without making them feel behind.

Right now, I think GitHub is sitting at the centre of one of the biggest behavioural shifts in technology — and I've unexpectedly found myself part of it.

I say that as someone who became part of the audience just a few weeks ago. Talk about timing.
